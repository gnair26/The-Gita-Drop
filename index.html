The story behind the text
The world's most famous conversation happened on a battlefield, between two people who really didn't want to be there.

The Bhagavad Gita is 18 chapters and 700 verses long. It was composed somewhere between 400 BCE and 200 CE. It has never, in two thousand years, stopped being relevant.

Picture this: two massive armies, face to face. Hundreds of thousands of soldiers. Chariots, war elephants, conches blaring. The greatest war in human history is about to begin.

In the middle of it all, one man asks his charioteer to drive him to the centre — to no man's land — so he can see both sides before the battle begins. When he gets there, he looks out and sees his teachers, his cousins, his uncles, his friends. On both sides.

His name is Arjuna. He is the greatest archer alive. And he drops his bow.

"My limbs fail and my mouth is parched. My body quivers and my hair stands on end. My bow slips from my hand... I am unable to stand. My mind seems to whirl."

— Arjuna, Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 1

His charioteer watches this calmly. Then begins to talk. That charioteer is Krishna — and what follows is one of the longest, most layered pep talks ever recorded.

But here's the thing: it is not about war. It never was. It's about duty, identity, fear, action, purpose, mortality, and what it means to be a human being who has to do hard things anyway. Arjuna just happened to need this conversation on a battlefield. Most of us need it at 9 pm on a Sunday before a difficult week.

The Gita asks one question, really: knowing what you have to do, will you show up? Every verse is just a different angle on that question. The Gita Drop delivers one angle, every morning.
