Chapter 1 of the Bhagavad Gita

Why Arjuna put down his bow

He was not afraid of dying. Arjuna was the greatest archer alive — death in battle held no fear for him. What broke him was looking across the battlefield and seeing who was standing on the other side.

"My limbs fail and my mouth is parched, my body quivers and my hair stands on end." — Arjuna, Chapter 1, Verse 29
👴
His Elders and Family
Bhishma (his great-uncle), Dhritarashtra (uncle), Drona (teacher) — people Arjuna was raised to honour and obey. Killing them, even in a just war, meant violating the deepest laws of dharma he knew.
📐
His Teachers
Dronacharya and Kripacharya. In the Brahminic tradition of Arjuna's time, one's guru was more sacred than one's parents. Raising a weapon against them was unthinkable. Yet they stood against him on the other side.
👥
His Cousins — All of Them
Duryodhana and the Kauravas were his first cousins — sons of his father Pandu's half-brother Dhritarashtra. They grew up together. He had known them his entire life.
☀️
A Brother He Didn't Know About
Karna was standing on the Kaurava side — and Arjuna did not yet know that Karna was his eldest half-brother, Kunti's firstborn. He knew Karna as a rival and an enemy. The full truth came too late.
🌍
The Consequences
Arjuna foresaw that killing the family elders would destroy the family order (kula dharma), produce illegitimate children, and cause generations of chaos. He was thinking about all of it at once, on a battlefield, with arrows about to fly.
📖
What Followed
Krishna's response to this crisis is the 700 verses of the Bhagavad Gita. He did not tell Arjuna to stop feeling it. He told him to understand it differently — and then to act anyway.
